# Whitepaper And Case Register

Use this for public whitepapers, case studies, case submissions, industry reports containing cases, and source material that an editorial or marketing team will later excerpt.

## Reader And Function

The reader wants to understand what happened, why it matters, what can be learned, and where the conclusion stops. The artifact should be credible enough to publish and modular enough to quote, without exposing internal production notes.

## Evidence Order

Keep these levels separate:

1. **Confirmed case fact**: what the company or project actually did.
2. **Observed result**: a number or visible change with a stated comparison basis.
3. **Case interpretation**: what the facts suggest about the mechanism.
4. **General method**: what another organization may reference under similar conditions.
5. **Editorial suggestion**: possible title, summary, chart, or excerpt.

Do not turn a proposed tactic, later idea, or adjacent project into a delivered case fact. A useful method can be inferred from the case, but label it as an interpretation or transferable condition rather than pretending it happened.

## Recommended Structure

- Case summary: the business situation and one central result.
- Problem: the specific behavior, process, or market constraint.
- Delivered actions: what was actually done, by whom, and at what point.
- Mechanism: why those actions could affect the result.
- Results: numbers, comparison period, and evidence boundary.
- Applicability: which organizations or conditions make the method relevant.
- Reusable editorial material: concise title, summary, chart notes, or approved quotations.

## Register Rules

- Use professional editorial Chinese, not internal project narration or sales promises.
- Prefer evidence-led judgments over capability labels.
- Keep necessary industry terms when they are defined by the surrounding facts.
- Do not force every sentence into everyday speech. A whitepaper may use analytical compression, but the verb-object relationship must remain natural.
- Avoid process debris such as file lists, version history, internal review notes, supplier roles, or reminders to the commissioning team unless the document is explicitly an internal source pack.
- Do not use "可复制、可迁移、方法论沉淀" without naming the conditions under which another organization could use the lesson.

## Common Failure Modes

- Writing a case as a capability brochure.
- Mixing delivered facts with unexecuted proposals.
- Treating one result number as proof of every claimed mechanism.
- Adding large material dumps instead of selecting evidence.
- Making the language so plain that the case loses analytical value.
- Making the language so polished that nobody can tell what was actually done.

## Final Check

- Can every delivered action be traced to a source?
- Are facts, interpretation, method, and editorial suggestions distinguishable?
- Does each result state its comparison basis or limitation?
- Can an external editor quote the material without seeing internal instructions?
- Does the article still sound like a whitepaper or case study rather than an internal memo or sales deck?
